RADIAL VELOCITY

The radial velocity method is a powerful tool for detecting exoplanets by measuring the subtle "wobble" of a star caused by an orbiting planet's gravitational pull. As the planet orbits, the star moves slightly, creating shifts in the star's light spectrum—toward blue when moving closer to Earth and toward red when moving away. These color shifts allow scientists to calculate the planet’s mass and orbit. This method has been particularly successful in discovering gas giants but can also detect smaller planets with sensitive instruments.

Radial velocity has played a key role in exoplanet discoveries, with many of the first-known exoplanets identified using this technique. Its strength lies in providing detailed information about a planet's characteristics, such as its minimum mass and orbit. Unlike the transit method, which requires specific alignments, radial velocity works even when planets don't pass in front of their stars, making it a versatile tool for exoplanet research.

Together with other methods like direct imaging and the transit technique, radial velocity remains a foundational approach for exoplanet discovery, helping to unveil the diversity of planets in our galaxy and providing a clearer picture of planetary formation and evolution.
