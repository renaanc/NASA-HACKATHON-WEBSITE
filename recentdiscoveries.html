EXOPLANET MISSIONS

NASA and ESA are leading several missions to discover and study exoplanets, helping us learn more about planets outside our solar system. NASA’s Kepler mission, launched in 2009, was a major breakthrough, discovering thousands of exoplanets using the transit method, which looks for tiny dips in starlight when a planet passes in front of its star. After Kepler, the TESS mission launched in 2018 to expand the search to nearby stars, focusing on finding smaller, rocky planets similar to Earth.

ESA, on the other hand, is leading a new wave of exoplanet research. Its CHEOPS mission, launched in 2019, aims to study known exoplanets by measuring their sizes with high precision, which helps scientists understand their composition. PLATO, set to launch in 2026, will search for Earth-like planets in habitable zones, where conditions may allow liquid water, a key ingredient for life. ESA's Ariel mission, planned for 2029, will analyze the atmospheres of a large number of exoplanets, providing insights into their chemistry, climate, and potential habitability.

These missions are important because they help us understand how planets form and evolve, and whether some might have conditions suitable for life. By working together, NASA and ESA are advancing our knowledge of exoplanets and bringing us closer to answering the age-old question: Are we alone in the universe?
